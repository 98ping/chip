## Why I stopped buying a new phone every year

Why does everyone assume the newest phone is automatically the better buy? I used to be that person — pre-ordered every fall, counted down to the keynote, the whole thing. Then I actually did the math, and the math is brutal.

Here's the thing: a flagship phone today is like a high-end gaming PC from three years ago. It was overkill the day you bought it. The camera bump gets a little nicer, the chip benchmarks a little higher, but be honest — when was the last time your phone felt *slow* doing the stuff you actually do? Texting, maps, music, a couple of apps. None of that needs this year's silicon.

Look, there are real reasons to upgrade. A battery that dies by 2 p.m., a screen you cracked months ago, a camera that genuinely can't keep up with what you need it for. Those are fair. "It's been twelve months" is not a reason — it's a marketing calendar. The phone companies need you on that calendar. You don't have to show up.

So I switched to one simple rule: upgrade when something breaks or stops doing its job, not when a keynote tells me to. Two years in, I've banked enough to cover a year of car insurance, and honestly? I couldn't tell you what this year's model does differently. That's the tell.
